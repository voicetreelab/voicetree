A folder takes the form of a section, with its own masthead and standfirst. Each child article sits on a ruled column inside that section. From the outside, the folder is approached as a single masthead — incoming edges meet the title, never the byline of a particular article.

Collapsed, the section folds into a see-also dispatch: italic title, Roman folio, count of articles within. The visual rhetoric is print, not screen — hairline rules, double borders, a single red ink that earns its place.

typography

Display set in DM Serif Display italic, drawing on 19th-c. American job-printing. Body in EB Garamond, a wide-set 16th-c. revival. All-caps labels in Antonio condensed sans, the cadence of column headers and dateline strips.

palette

Ivory paper #F7F4EA, printer's black #0E0D0A. Exactly one red — vermilion #B8281C — used as the editor's mark: status dots, asterisms, leader caps. No second color competes.

motion

The collapse fold is a typeset re-paginate: the section turns to a see-also dispatch over ~280ms. Hovering a child article surfaces a hairline underline, never a glow. Edges trace themselves like leader rules from masthead to masthead.

edge interface

The folder publishes one point of contact — its masthead. Cross-folder edges arrive at the title, never at a member article. Collapsed sections offer a folio reference rather than a member preview.

information density

Members are sized by editorial priority, not by content length. The folder section is paginated with vertical ruling so a glance reveals row alignment. Whitespace earns its keep, in print as on screen.

departures

This is the most maximalist direction: typographic, deliberately literary. It celebrates the act of folding as an editorial decision, not a UI affordance. Adopt fully or not at all — half-prints look like a brochure.
